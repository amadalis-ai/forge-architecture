How Forge Works

A visual guide to what makes Forge fundamentally different from every other AI agent platform — and why that difference matters.

Before We Start

Three concepts you need to know

These words come up throughout this document. Here's what they actually mean — no jargon, no hand-waving.

🏠

Sandbox

A sealed room where the AI does its work

Imagine giving someone a private office. They get a desk, a computer, and exactly the tools they need for the job. They can do anything inside that room — write documents, run calculations, build things. But they can't walk out and access the rest of the building unless you explicitly open a door.

If they make a mess, the mess stays in the room. If they try to reach something they're not supposed to, the walls stop them. When the job is done, you collect what they produced, verify it, and the room resets for the next person.

Why it matters: This is what makes autonomous AI safe for enterprise use. The AI has real freedom to work — but physical boundaries, not just promises to behave.
📝

Code

How the AI takes action instead of just talking

When you ask a chatbot "what's the average revenue across these 500 customers?" — it reads the question and predicts what the answer probably looks like. It might be right. It might be wrong. You can't tell.

When Forge's AI answers the same question, it doesn't guess. It writes a small program — maybe ten lines — that actually opens the data file, reads every row, adds up the numbers, divides by the count, and prints the result. That program runs inside the sandbox. The answer comes from computation, not prediction.

Why it matters: Code is verifiable. If the AI says "the average is $47,000" and it wrote a program to compute that, you can read the program and confirm it did the right math. If it just said "$47,000" in conversation, you have no idea if that's real or made up.
💭

Thinking

The AI pauses and plans before it acts

Most AI responds immediately — you ask, it answers. That's fast, but it's shallow. The AI that does Forge's planning step uses "extended thinking" — it literally works through the problem internally for 30–60 seconds before producing an answer.

It considers what needs to happen, in what order, what could go wrong, what each step needs from the previous one. It's the difference between someone who blurts out an answer and someone who pauses, thinks it through, and then gives you a structured plan.

Why it matters: Planning is where the expensive AI earns its cost. One minute of deep thinking prevents twenty minutes of failed execution and wasted money downstream.

Every other platform lets the code control the AI.
Forge lets the AI control the code.

02 — Why "Compiled" Matters

Other platforms hope things work.
Forge validates before it runs.

The word "compiled" comes from how programming languages work. It means: check everything for errors before you start, not while you're running.

The Interpreter Pattern

Figure it out as you go

The AI gets a task. It tries step 1. Looks at the result. Tries step 2. Looks at the result. Tries step 3.

If step 8 needs something from step 3 that doesn't exist — the system finds out at step 8. After steps 1–7 already cost money and time.

It's like starting a road trip without checking if the roads exist.

vs
The Compiler Pattern

Verify everything first, then run

The AI plans the entire task upfront. The system checks: Do all the tools exist? Do the inputs and outputs connect? Are the skills available?

If anything is missing, the system catches it before any money is spent. Only verified plans execute.

It's like verifying every road, gas station, and hotel before you leave the driveway.

Why this saves money

With the interpreter pattern, a failed task at step 8 means you already paid for steps 1–7. With Forge, a plan that can't work is rejected before step 1 runs. You pay for thinking (cheap) instead of paying for failing (expensive).

03 — How a Task Flows Through Forge

From a user's request to a verified deliverable

When someone asks Forge to do something, this is what happens behind the scenes:

🧠

Planning

A powerful AI thinks deeply about the task. It produces a structured plan with steps, dependencies, and success criteria. No tools yet — just "what needs to happen."

🔍

Capability Discovery

The system (not the AI) searches for the skills and tools each step needs. If a capability doesn't exist, it can't be used. No hallucinated tools.

🔗

Binding

The AI selects from only what was discovered. It can't reference tools that don't exist. Like a spell-checker for capabilities.

⚙️

Compilation

The plan is locked down. Every step gets a unique ID, declared inputs/outputs, and a contract hash. Nothing changes after this point.

🔒

Governed Execution

Each step runs in a fresh, clean environment with only what it needs. The platform handles files, security, and error recovery. The AI just does the work.

🔎

Post-Step Audit

After each step, the platform mechanically verifies: Do the expected files exist? Do the checksums match? Then a separate AI reviews the actual work — are the files empty? Does the HTML actually contain HTML? Does the analysis make sense? If something looks wrong, it flags it before the next step starts.

✅

Verified Delivery

Only work that passed both mechanical checks and AI review reaches the user. Every deliverable has a postflight receipt — what was produced, when, by which step, verified by whom.

05 — Why Our Workflows Are Different

Traditional workflows are train tracks.
Forge workflows are destinations with freedom.

Most workflow tools force the AI onto rigid rails. Forge gives it a destination and lets it drive.

Traditional Workflows

Rigid rails

"Go to step A. If condition X, go to step B. If condition Y, go to step C. If anything unexpected happens, the workflow breaks."

Every edge case needs another branch. The workflow becomes an infinitely expanding decision tree.

The developer does all the thinking upfront. The AI just fills in boxes.

vs
Forge's Compiled Plans

Destinations with contracts

"Step 1: achieve this objective. Step 2: take step 1's output and achieve this objective." Each step has clear criteria for success.

The AI writes whatever code it needs to achieve each objective. It adapts to unexpected data, new formats, edge cases.

A traditional workflow says "turn left, then right, then straight for 2 miles." If there's construction, the workflow fails. Forge says "get to the restaurant by 7pm" and verifies you arrived. How you get there is up to the driver — who happens to be very smart.

06 — Each Step Gets a Fresh Mind

No baggage. No confusion. Just one job.

This is a subtle but critical difference. When Forge runs a multi-step plan, each step is executed by a fresh AI — not one that's been accumulating confusion from the previous steps.

07 — When This Really Matters

At scale, unreliable AI isn't just annoying.
It's expensive and dangerous.

These are real scenarios where the difference between "hope it works" and "compiled and verified" isn't academic — it's the difference between a useful system and one that can't be trusted.

1,000
resumes

Screening a thousand candidates for a hiring pipeline

A recruiting team needs to screen 1,000 resumes against a job description, score each candidate on 8 criteria, and produce a ranked spreadsheet with justifications. That's a 40+ step plan with hundreds of sub-steps — fetching files, extracting text, parsing skills, computing scores, writing summaries, compiling rankings.

Without compilation: By resume 300, the model has accumulated so much context that its scoring drifts. Candidate 50 and candidate 850 are scored on different mental scales. The model starts hallucinating skills it saw in earlier resumes. By the end, the rankings are unreliable — but nobody knows because the numbers look plausible.
With Forge: Each batch of resumes is processed by a fresh agent with clean context — no drift, no accumulated confusion. Every score is computed by code, not predicted from memory. The post-step audit verifies that every resume produced a score, that no scores are missing, and that the justification text isn't empty. The output is trustworthy because it was computed, not guessed.
200
contracts

Extracting key terms from vendor contracts for compliance review

A legal team needs to extract termination clauses, liability caps, governing law, auto-renewal terms, and SLA commitments from 200 vendor contracts — then flag deviations from the company's standard terms. Each contract is different: different formats, different structures, different language.

Without compilation: The model summarizes each contract conversationally. It tells you "the termination clause requires 30 days notice" — but did it actually read Section 7.2, or did it predict what a termination clause probably says? There's no citation, no extraction, no structural validation. You can't tell hallucinated findings from real ones.
With Forge: Each contract is processed through code that structurally extracts clauses, cross-references against the standard template, and produces findings with specific section citations. The audit step verifies that every required field was extracted — not just that a file was produced, but that the file contains actual structured data with non-empty values. If the extraction missed a field, it's flagged before the finding reaches the compliance team.
50
data sources

Building a competitive landscape report from 50 sources

A strategy team asks for a competitive analysis across 12 competitors, pulling from financial filings, press releases, product pages, review sites, and job postings. The final deliverable is a 30-page report with charts, a comparison matrix, and sourced citations — plus a presentation deck.

Without compilation: The model starts researching. By step 15, it's forgotten the constraints from step 2. It uses data it gathered in step 4 but misattributes it to a different competitor. It produces charts with numbers it didn't compute — it predicted them. The report looks professional. The numbers in it can't be verified because there's no trail from source to finding.
With Forge: Each competitor is researched by a fresh agent that produces a structured profile with sourced data. The compilation pipeline wires each profile into the synthesis step as a declared input artifact. The agent that writes the comparison matrix receives verified, checksummed inputs — not stale memories. Every number in a chart was computed by code from data that exists in a file. The audit step confirms that every chart references real data, every citation points to a real source, and no section of the report is suspiciously empty.
5
years of data

Financial analysis: computing quarterly metrics across 5 years of data

A finance team needs revenue growth rates, gross margin trends, customer acquisition costs, and LTV:CAC ratios computed across 20 quarters — from raw data, not from the model's memory of what those numbers "probably" are.

Without compilation: The model tells you "Q3 2024 revenue growth was 18%." But did it compute 18% from the actual data, or did it predict what revenue growth probably looks like for a company of this size? If you can't see the calculation, you can't trust the number. One wrong growth rate cascades through every downstream metric.
With Forge: The model writes Python code that reads the actual data files, performs the actual arithmetic, and outputs the results. The code is visible and auditable. The post-step audit verifies that every quarter has values, that no quarter was skipped, and that the computed totals are internally consistent. A second AI reviewer checks whether the numbers are plausible given the inputs. The finance team gets numbers they can trace from formula to source file.
The pattern

In every one of these scenarios, the problem isn't that AI can't do the work. AI is extraordinarily capable. The problem is that without structure, verification, and code-based computation, you can't trust that the work was done correctly. Forge doesn't make AI smarter. It makes AI trustworthy.
